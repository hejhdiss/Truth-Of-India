# The Broken Promise: How BJP and RSS Betrayed India's Farmers

*A Critical Investigative Report | Sources: National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), Reuters, BBC, Al Jazeera, The Wire, Scroll.in, Article 14, The Hindu, Frontline, P. Sainath / People's Archive of Rural India (PARI), Amnesty International, UN Special Rapporteur on the Right to Food, Ministry of Agriculture data, Parliamentary Standing Committee reports*

---

## Introduction

India's farmers feed 1.4 billion people. They comprise nearly 44% of the country's workforce. They are the foundation on which every Indian life rests.

Under BJP and RSS rule since 2014, this foundation has been systematically cracked. The evidence is not in opposition pamphlets — it is in the suicide data published by India's own government, in the debt figures compiled by the Reserve Bank of India, in the tears of the 700,000 farmers who marched to Delhi in the winter of 2020 and sat in the cold for over a year, and in the bodies of the protesters who died at Lakhimpur Kheri when a vehicle driven by the son of a Union minister ran them over.

Modi came to power in 2014 promising to double farmers' income by 2022. In 2022, the deadline passed in silence. No government official mentioned it. No accounting was done. The farmers were left with the same crushing debts, the same broken markets, and the same contempt from the men who had once asked for their votes.

## Part 1: The Farmer Suicide Epidemic

### Numbers the Government Cannot Hide

Every year, the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) publishes data on "accidental deaths and suicides in India." Buried inside this report, year after year, is a number that should stop the country cold.

According to NCRB data, **more than 300,000 farmers and agricultural labourers have died by suicide in India since 1995** — one of the largest recorded waves of farmer suicides in human history. In 2022 alone, 11,290 people engaged in farming activities died by suicide — an average of **30 farmers every single day**.

These are not natural deaths. They are deaths of despair, driven by a documented set of causes: crushing debt to moneylenders, crop failures uninsured against weather and price collapse, the gap between the cost of farming and the price offered for crops, and the complete absence of a safety net.

Journalist and Magsaysay Award winner P. Sainath, who has documented farmer distress for three decades through the People's Archive of Rural India, describes the crisis as "a policy-induced holocaust." The policies that created it — trade liberalisation that crashed crop prices, the withdrawal of rural credit forcing farmers into moneylender debt, the destruction of buffer stock systems — did not begin with BJP. But the BJP government accelerated them, and when farmers organised to resist, the response was tear gas, sedition charges, and a vehicle that drove into protesters.

### The Debt Trap

The Reserve Bank of India's own data shows that the average Indian farm household carries debt of approximately **₹74,121** — a figure that, relative to average agricultural income, represents a debt burden from which most families cannot escape without a complete write-off or a complete crop failure.

The formal banking system has retreated from rural India. Under BJP governance, the share of institutional credit to agriculture has stagnated while rural moneylending — at interest rates of 24% to 60% annually — has filled the gap. When a crop fails, the debt compounds faster than any harvest can repay it. The farmer has one asset: land. When that goes to the moneylender, the family has nothing. Across India, in Maharashtra's Vidarbha, in Telangana, in Punjab, in Rajasthan, the pattern is the same.

## Part 2: The Three Farm Laws — A Betrayal Written in Legislation

### What the Laws Actually Did

In September 2020, at the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, with Parliament in a truncated session and no committee review, the BJP government pushed through three agricultural reform laws in a single day:

- **The Farmers' Produce Trade and Commerce (Promotion and Facilitation) Act** — allowed corporations to buy crops outside of the regulated mandi system, removing the guaranteed floor for mandi prices
- **The Farmers (Empowerment and Protection) Agreement on Price Assurance and Farm Services Act** — enabled contract farming on terms that farmers and legal experts said strongly favoured corporate buyers
- **The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act** — removed storage limits on food commodities, allowing large corporations to stockpile food grains without regulation

The government presented these as liberalisation that would help farmers access better markets. Farmers across Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, and Rajasthan read them differently — and they were right to. Agricultural economists at multiple Indian universities pointed out that the laws created a two-track system: a regulated mandi track where Minimum Support Price (MSP) existed, and a deregulated corporate track where it did not. Over time, as corporations diverted purchasing to the unregulated track, the mandi system — and with it, price guarantees — would wither.

Farmers demanded one thing above all: **make MSP a legal guarantee.** The government refused.

### The Protest: 700,000 People, One Year, 700 Deaths

Beginning in November 2020, hundreds of thousands of farmers — predominantly from Punjab, Haryana, and western Uttar Pradesh — marched to Delhi and established protest camps at three border points: Singhu, Tikri, and Ghazipur. At its peak, the protest involved an estimated **700,000 people**.

They sat through winter temperatures below 5°C. They built libraries, medical camps, and community kitchens at the protest sites. They held lectures, cultural programs, and prayer meetings. They remained disciplined and peaceful for over a year.

The government's response was to:

- **Label them terrorists and Khalistanis** — Home Minister Amit Shah and multiple BJP leaders used the word "Khalistani" repeatedly, accusing the predominantly Sikh farmers from Punjab of separatism, with no evidence
- **Cut internet and mobile services** at protest sites on multiple occasions
- **Erect concrete barriers, barbed wire, and iron nails** hammered into the roads leading to Delhi to prevent tractors from approaching
- **File sedition charges** against journalists and activists who covered the protests sympathetically, including Disha Ravi, a 21-year-old climate activist who shared a protest toolkit document online and was arrested

In total, according to farmer unions, **over 700 protesters died** during the course of the year-long agitation — from cold, illness, road accidents, and the physical toll of months of outdoor living. No government minister visited the protest sites to express condolences. No official statement acknowledged the deaths.

### Lakhimpur Kheri: When the State Ran Farmers Over

On October 3, 2021, a convoy of vehicles carrying Union Minister of State for Home Affairs Ajay Mishra Teni — one of the most senior BJP ministers — arrived in Lakhimpur Kheri, Uttar Pradesh, where farmers were protesting.

A vehicle in the convoy — driven by Ashish Mishra, the minister's son — drove directly into a crowd of protesting farmers. **Four farmers were killed, along with a journalist and a driver.** Video footage documented the vehicle driving into the crowd. The footage was widely circulated.

Ashish Mishra was eventually arrested — but only after the Supreme Court took suo motu notice and expressed "shock" at the UP government's failure to act. He was granted bail within months. His father, Union Minister Ajay Mishra Teni, was **not removed from cabinet**. He continued to serve as a Union Minister of State for Home Affairs — the ministry responsible for law and order — for over a year after his son killed farmers with a vehicle.

The Supreme Court-appointed committee reviewing the case called the UP government's investigation "unsatisfactory." The message to farmers and to the country was clear: a minister's son can kill you with a car and your government will not even remove his father from office.

### The Repeal: A Victory Without Justice

In November 2021, after a year of protest and with state elections approaching in Punjab and Uttar Pradesh, Prime Minister Modi announced the repeal of all three farm laws. He offered no apology for the 700 deaths. No compensation was announced for families. No inquiry was ordered into Lakhimpur Kheri beyond the court-monitored one already underway.

The government had been forced to retreat by the largest sustained peaceful protest in Indian history. It gave nothing except the bare minimum — the withdrawal of the laws — and then asked farmers to go home. Farmers stayed until February 2022 for written assurances on MSP. No written assurances came.

The demand for legal MSP guarantee — the one demand at the heart of the entire protest — was never met.

## Part 3: The Broken Promise — Doubling Farmers' Income

### Modi's 2022 Deadline, Quietly Buried

In February 2016, Prime Minister Modi announced the government's flagship agricultural goal: **doubling farmers' income by 2022**, the 75th year of Indian independence. It became a central BJP campaign promise, repeated in speeches and manifestos for years.

In 2022, the deadline arrived. A parliamentary committee asked the Ministry of Agriculture for data showing whether farmers' incomes had doubled. The ministry's response, documented in the committee's report, was that it **did not have reliable data** to confirm whether the target had been achieved.

Independent agricultural economists, including researchers at the International Food Policy Research Institute (IFPRI) and India's own National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development (NABARD), found no evidence that real farm incomes had doubled. After adjusting for inflation, the increase in farm incomes between 2015 and 2022 was well below the 100% target. In many states and crop categories, real incomes had effectively stagnated or declined.

The government simply stopped talking about it.

### PM-KISAN: Too Little, Exposed as Inadequate

The BJP's primary response to farm distress was the PM-KISAN scheme, launched in 2019, which provides ₹6,000 per year (₹500 per month) directly to farmer bank accounts. The scheme reaches tens of millions of households and is genuinely a transfer — the money reaches farmers.

But ₹500 per month — approximately $6 USD — does not come close to addressing the structural crisis. The average cost of cultivation for one acre of wheat in Punjab is approximately ₹20,000–25,000. The average Indian farm holding is 1.1 hectares. The entire annual PM-KISAN transfer does not cover a fraction of a single season's input costs. It is not a solution. It is an electoral placebo.

## Part 4: Corporatisation of Agriculture — Who the Farm Laws Really Served

### The Adani-Agriculture Connection

The farm laws that farmers feared most — the removal of regulated mandi infrastructure and the deregulation of food storage — would have benefited, above all, corporations with large warehouse and logistics networks. Among India's largest holders of grain storage and logistics infrastructure: the Adani Group.

Adani Agri Logistics, a subsidiary of Adani Group, holds long-term contracts to manage government grain storage and transportation. The deregulation of food commodity storage limits under the Essential Commodities Amendment Act would have significantly expanded the commercial advantage of entities with large warehousing capacity — precisely the kind of infrastructure Adani Group has systematically built with government contract support.

Farmers noticed. Their protest signs, in multiple languages, named Adani and Ambani explicitly. The government called this a conspiracy theory. But no one explained why laws ostensibly designed to help farmers were written in a way that primarily benefited corporations — and why the one demand farmers universally made, MSP legalisation, was the one demand the government would never grant.

## Conclusion: The Land They Stand On

India's farmers are not poor because farming is hard. Farming is hard everywhere. They are poor because for decades, and accelerating under BJP governance, policy has been systematically structured to extract from rural India and deliver to urban corporate India — cheap food kept cheap by suppressing farmers' prices, cheap labour kept cheap by keeping rural areas poor.

The BJP came to power in 2014 promising to reverse this. "Jai Jawan, Jai Kisan" — the slogan that defined post-independence India's respect for soldiers and farmers — was replaced in practice by a government that called Sikh farmers terrorists, erected iron nails in roads to stop their tractors, and retained in cabinet a minister whose son killed protesters with a car.

Over 300,000 farmer suicides since 1995. Thirty deaths every day in 2022. Seven hundred dead at the gates of Delhi. Not one apology.

**This is the BJP's agricultural record. Every number is from the government's own data.**
