# Digital India, Disrupted: How BJP and RSS Policies Are Undermining India's Tech Future

*A Critical Investigative Report | Sources: Human Rights Watch, Freedom House, Access Now, Tandfonline (Democratization Journal), Time, Reuters Institute, CNN, Al Jazeera, NPR, The Wire, Endangered Scholars Worldwide*

---

## Introduction

India markets itself to the world as a rising digital superpower — the land of "Digital India," 114 unicorn startups, and the world's largest biometric identity system. Prime Minister Narendra Modi's Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) government has championed technology as the backbone of its development narrative.

But beneath this optimistic headline lies a more troubling reality. A growing body of evidence from academic researchers, international human rights organisations, press freedom watchdogs, and technology policy analysts reveals that the BJP government — and its ideological parent, the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS) — have enacted policies and created conditions that systematically damage the very tech ecosystem they claim to champion.

This article examines four key areas where BJP and RSS influence has provoked documented harm to India's technological development: internet shutdowns, digital censorship and repressive IT laws, the assault on academic freedom and scientific research, and the erosion of investor confidence.

---

## Part 1: The Internet Kill Switch — India's Shutdown Epidemic

India has, for five consecutive years, held the shameful distinction of being the world's number-one executor of internet shutdowns.

According to Access Now and the #KeepItOn coalition, **in 2022 alone, India was responsible for 84 of the 187 internet shutdowns recorded globally** — meaning India accounted for nearly half of the world's total. Meta separately counted 41 intentional internet disruptions inside India in 2021, more than any other nation on earth.

The economic toll is severe. Top10VPN, a digital privacy research organisation, estimated the total cost of shutdowns in India at **$184.3 million in 2022 alone** — and that figure is acknowledged to be an undercount, as it excludes the informal sector and unreported shutdowns. In Kashmir, a single shutdown in 2019 left an estimated 500,000 people unemployed, primarily in the tourism sector, according to the Kashmir Chambers of Commerce and Industry.

The Human Rights Watch documented the human consequences bluntly: *"Digital India has become a way of life particularly for the poor, marginalized and for those in government."* Yet it is those same marginalised people who suffer most when the plug is pulled. Without internet, citizens cannot access government food programmes, banking services, or digital work — all the services the BJP's "Digital India" initiative pushed them to use in the first place. As technology lawyer Mishi Choudhary of the Software Freedom Law Center put it: *"On the one hand, the government pushes us all to live online, and on the other, it maintains a kill switch and uses it often. And once you use that, you bring the entire economy and the entire social services to its knees."*

### The BJP Fingerprint

Crucially, this is not a politically neutral phenomenon. A landmark peer-reviewed study published in the journal *Democratization* by Dr. Kris Ruijgrok of the University of Amsterdam, funded by the Open Technology Fund, provides quantitative proof of the BJP's role. Ruijgrok's data analysis found that **the rise of India's internet shutdowns runs in direct parallel with the BJP's rise to national power**, and that shutdowns occur primarily in BJP-ruled states.

Specifically, the study found that between 2012 and 2020, **on average 9.6 districts per month experienced internet shutdowns in BJP-governed states — 3.5 times the rate of 2.7 districts per month in states without BJP rule**. A district in a BJP-run state had a 3% probability of having its internet cut, versus only 0.8% in non-BJP states.

The shutdowns are clearly triggered by political events. When BJP-ruled states cracked down on protesters opposing the Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) in 2019 — a law that excluded Muslims from citizenship protections — 14 of the 19 reported internet shutdowns were ordered in BJP-governed states. At least 30 people were killed during the protests, all in BJP states.

In Manipur in 2023, the BJP state government cut internet access for what became one of India's longest shutdowns, as ethnic violence spiralled. Digital rights activists told *Time* magazine there was no evidence the shutdown reduced violence — it only prevented information from reaching the outside world, delaying national and international response to the crisis.

Even India's own Parliamentary Standing Committee on Communications and Information Technology concluded in 2021 that the government's suspension rules had been *"grossly misused leading to huge economic loss and also causing untold suffering to the public."* The government's response to this damning assessment? The Ministry of Electronics and IT told Parliament in 2022 that it does not even maintain centralised records of shutdowns.

## Part 2: The Censorship Architecture — IT Rules, App Bans, and the Silencing of Critics

The BJP government has constructed a legal architecture for digital censorship that critics across the political spectrum and in multiple democracies have condemned as incompatible with free expression.

### The IT Rules 2021

The Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules 2021 — commonly known as the IT Rules — introduced by the BJP government gave authorities sweeping powers over online speech. The International Press Institute (IPI) described these rules as frequently *"deployed to punish and restrict any criticism of India's ruling government."*

Under the rules, social media platforms with over five million users were required to remove "unlawful" content — defined expansively to include anything affecting public order, decency, morality, national sovereignty, or security. The vague wording gave the government enormous latitude to order takedowns of political criticism. Critically, company executives in India can be held **criminally liable** for non-compliance, a provision Freedom House's research analyst Kian Vesteinsson described as a deliberate tactic: *"forcing companies to put their staff on the line to protect free speech is a tactic that governments increasingly use to tighten their grasp over online expression."*

The rules also sought to undermine end-to-end encryption by requiring messaging platforms to identify the "first originator" of messages — effectively breaking the privacy protections of services like WhatsApp. The Internet Society called this provision an attack on user privacy, data confidentiality, and the principle of data minimisation.

Freedom House's *Freedom on the Net 2024* report documented that in 2023, India blocked 7,502 websites and accounts — up from 6,935 the previous year. During the 2024 election period, authorities ordered removal of content critical of the BJP. The report noted that the BJP *"increasingly used government institutions to target political opponents"* while online information was manipulated by BJP campaigns.

### Intimidation of Tech Giants and Independent Media

The BJP government's use of these laws against tech companies has been well documented by NPR, Reuters Institute, and others. In one revealing episode, BJP politicians tweeted what India's most reputable fact-checkers confirmed was a forged document — a fake "media toolkit" supposedly from the opposition. Twitter labelled the tweets as manipulated media. The government demanded the label be removed. When Twitter refused, **police raided its offices three days later**.

In 2023, the government used emergency powers under the IT Rules to ban a BBC documentary, *India: The Modi Question*, which examined Modi's leadership during the 2002 Gujarat riots in which around 1,000 people — mostly Muslims — were killed. Twitter and YouTube complied with the government's blocking orders. Multiple press freedom organisations condemned the ban.

The Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism documented a crackdown on independent YouTube news channels during the 2024 elections. Hindi news channel *Bolta Hindustan*, with 275,000 subscribers, was blocked without explanation after an order from the Ministry of Information and Broadcasting. Its founder, Haseen Rehmani, was not told why his channel, which had posted over 4,000 videos, was removed.

According to Human Rights Watch, *"authorities in BJP-led Uttar Pradesh state have repeatedly filed false charges against journalists for publishing content and social media posts critical of the government. Since 2017, after BJP leader Yogi Adityanath became the state's chief minister, the authorities have filed criminal cases against 66 journalists."*

### The RSS and Digital Content Control

The RSS's fingerprints are also on the censorship architecture aimed at creative and digital industries. A 2024 academic paper published in the *International Journal of Cultural Policy* (Taylor & Francis) documented that in 2019, the RSS held meetings with Netflix and Amazon Prime Video executives demanding restrictions on content deemed *"anti-Hindu and anti-national."* RSS affiliates Bharatiya Chitra Sadhana and Sanskar Bharati met with scriptwriters and online content curators, pressuring them to change their narratives. BJP spokesperson Tajinder Bagga filed a police complaint against acclaimed director Anurag Kashyap over his series *Sacred Games*, alleging it insulted a religious community.

The paper concluded that IT Rules 2021 *"connect state and mob censorship, where citizens are recruited"* into the enforcement of ideological conformity.

## Part 3: Killing the Mind — RSS and the Assault on Academic and Scientific Freedom

Technology is built on scientific inquiry and critical thought. It is here — in India's universities, research institutes, and innovation labs — that the BJP-RSS combine has inflicted some of its most lasting structural damage.

### India's Academic Freedom Collapse

India's score on the Global Academic Freedom Index has collapsed from **0.66 in 2012 to just 0.16 in 2024** — a catastrophic decline that tracks almost exactly with the BJP's time in power since 2014.

The International Studies Association warned in April 2024 that *"academic freedom and institutional autonomy are being systematically eroded by political control and interference by the state and ruling parties."* The Boundary 2 Journal described how India's intellectual spaces are being *"re-engineered to reward conformity and punish critical scholarship."*

### RSS Control of Research Bodies

Since 2014, the BJP government has placed individuals with RSS backgrounds at the heads of India's premier research funding bodies. The Indian Council for Historical Research (ICHR) chair appointed in 2014 was a member of the RSS History Wing with no peer-reviewed publications. The Indian Council for Social Science Research (ICSSR) chair appointed in 2017 was a founding member of an RSS front organisation. The Indian Council for Philosophical Research (ICPR) organised a seminar glorifying RSS ideologue M.S. Golwalkar and cancelled a seminar that included papers on indigenous religions.

In 2020, the Ministry of Science and Technology — along with several research bodies — backed an inter-ministerial funding programme called SUTRA-PIC, which directed science ministry resources toward studying the properties of indigenous cows. At the 2015 Indian Science Congress, a government-allowed symposium promoted pseudoscientific claims about ancient Indian aviation technology.

As researchers at *The India Forum* documented, government-funded research in BJP-ruled states has been redirected toward praising ruling party policy. In Gujarat, the government issued a list of 82 PhD thesis topics all focused on the "successful execution" of state and central government schemes. At Lucknow University, exam questions were set about Modi's governance programmes.

The Wire reported that major public funding bodies including the ICSSR now increasingly prioritise research aligned with the BJP's "Viksit Bharat" (Developed India) initiative, while *"conferences or research agendas that fall outside this framework... struggle to secure funding."*

### IIMs, IITs, and the Hollowing of India's Premier Institutions

India's elite institutions — the IITs and IIMs — are not immune. As independent journalist Abhisar Sharma noted in an interview with Newslaundry, the BJP and RSS are *"tightening control over universities — from JNU and BHU to IITs and IIMs — eroding their independent character."*

Three IIM directors resigned within five years following clashes with politically influenced boards. Former IIM Calcutta directors Anju Seth (2021) and Uttam Kumar Sarkar (2023) resigned over board interference. The most recent departure cited *"constriction of professional space."* A Chhattisgarh High Court ruling found that the IIM Act 2017 — passed under the BJP — effectively reduced directors to figureheads by giving disciplinary authority to government-appointed boards.

At IIT Delhi, a conference titled *"Critical Philosophy of Caste and Race"* faced intense political pressure and administrative hostility. Delhi University cancelled a seminar on democracy while simultaneously issuing a directive to promote a government cow welfare summit. The university's standing academic committee asked the Department of Political Science to remove topics including *"religious nationalism"* and *"the politics of the RSS"* from its curriculum.

In December 2025, opposition leader Rahul Gandhi alleged that the RSS was conducting an institutional takeover of the Indian Statistical Institute (ISI), one of India's most prestigious mathematics and statistics research bodies — a charge that, if accurate, would have direct implications for India's data science and AI talent pipeline.

## Part 4: Investor Flight and the Innovation Deficit

The cumulative effect of digital repression, censorship, regulatory intimidation, and weakened research institutions shows up in hard economic data.

India's **net Foreign Direct Investment collapsed by 96.5% in FY2025**, from $10.1 billion the previous year to just $353 million. The drop was attributed to a dramatic increase in repatriation — $49 billion was withdrawn from India in FY25. Tech industry veteran Mohandas Pai pointed to government inaction as a contributing factor: *"I think it is important for regulators like the RBI and SEBI to ease the process of accessing funds."*

India's startup funding fell over 62% in 2023. Fintech funding plunged 63%. Deep-tech startups — the kind that generate breakthrough technologies — received only $1.6 billion in 2024, a fraction of what China or the United States invest in comparable sectors. In 2024, **Indian startups raised just $30.4 billion in total — dwarfed by the $845 billion raised by Chinese firms** over a comparable period.

The innovation gap is not a coincidence. It tracks directly with the suppression of the universities, research institutions, and critical inquiry that produce the next generation of technology entrepreneurs and scientists. When the government funds cow research instead of quantum computing, when IIM directors resign rather than face political interference, when fact-checkers are arrested under IT Rules, the downstream damage to innovation is inevitable.

Policy analysts also identify regulatory bottlenecks — including what was until recently an "angel tax" on startup investments and compliance overloads — as discouraging innovation at the foundational level.

---

## Conclusion: The Contradiction at the Heart of "Digital India"

The BJP government's "Digital India" programme has achieved real results in infrastructure: over 149,000 public Wi-Fi hotspots, a 5G rollout, and a UPI payments system processing over 100 billion transactions annually. These are genuine achievements that deserve acknowledgment.

But those achievements are being undermined by the same government's other hand. A country cannot build a world-class digital economy while simultaneously shutting down the internet more than any other nation on earth. It cannot attract global tech investment while using criminal law to coerce Silicon Valley companies into compliance with censorship orders. It cannot produce world-leading scientists when the RSS is directing PhD topics and cancelling seminars on democracy.

The data is unambiguous. India's internet shutdowns are concentrated in BJP-ruled states. Its censorship laws are used overwhelmingly against BJP critics. Its research institutions are being staffed with RSS loyalists. Its academic freedom score has cratered. Its FDI has fled.

"Digital India" — as currently constructed — is not a technology development programme. It is a branding exercise built on a foundation that the BJP and RSS are actively eroding from beneath.
