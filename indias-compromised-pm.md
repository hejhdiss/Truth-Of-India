## India Has Had Leaders Who Made the World Nervous

Let us begin with what India used to look like from Washington and London.

**Jawaharlal Nehru** co-founded the Non-Aligned Movement — a geopolitical declaration that India would not be a pawn of either the US or the Soviet Union. In the middle of the Cold War, when every country was being pressured to pick a side, India said: *we pick our own side.* That was sovereignty.

**Indira Gandhi**, December 1971. The United States sent the USS Enterprise — a nuclear-powered aircraft carrier — into the Bay of Bengal to intimidate India into stopping its advance in the Bangladesh Liberation War. Nixon privately called Indians "slippery, treacherous people" and worse. Gandhi's response was to sign a mutual defense treaty with the Soviet Union, who then dispatched nuclear-armed submarines to shadow the American fleet. The USS Enterprise turned around. India liberated Bangladesh. 93,000 Pakistani soldiers surrendered in the largest military surrender since World War II.

That was a Prime Minister with a spinal cord.

**V.P. Singh** took on the entrenched upper-caste establishment with the Mandal Commission implementation — knowing it would politically destroy him. It did. He did it anyway. Spine.

**Atal Bihari Vajpayee** — a BJP Prime Minister, let it be noted — conducted the Pokhran nuclear tests in 1998 against enormous American pressure and threats of sanctions. He looked Washington in the eye and said India will determine its own security. America sanctioned India. Vajpayee didn't reverse course. Spine.

Even **P.V. Narasimha Rao**, often underestimated, quietly authorized India's nuclear weapons program development while maintaining diplomatic composure internationally. Spine, of a quieter variety.

These were leaders — of different parties, different ideologies, different religions, different regions — who understood one fundamental thing: **India is not anyone's junior partner.**

## And Then There Was the Chaiwala

Narendra Modi came to power in 2014 on a wave of manufactured nationalism, promising a strong India, a decisive India, a globally respected India.

*"56-inch chest,"* his supporters said. A strongman. Finally.

Let us examine what that chest has actually done when it mattered.

**On Palestine:** India has historically supported Palestinian rights at the UN. Under Modi, India's position quietly shifted — abstaining on or softening votes that previous governments would have supported clearly. While Gaza was being bombed, Modi flew to Israel, embraced Netanyahu warmly, and described India and Israel as natural partners. Not a word about the children dying in the rubble.

**On the US:** Can anyone name a single instance where Modi said no to Washington on a matter of principle? A single foreign policy decision where India contradicted American interests in a meaningful way? The Non-Aligned Movement — that Nehru built and India once led — has been quietly abandoned in practice, replaced by "strategic partnership" language that means, in plain terms: we will not embarrass you publicly.

**On oil — the comic masterpiece of our times, now with full documentation:**

Here is the sequence of events, reported by Reuters, Bloomberg, CNBC, and every major financial outlet simultaneously:

**2025:** America slapped a 25% "penalty" tariff on India for buying Russian oil. India — the world's fourth largest refiner — quietly began reducing Russian oil purchases to avoid the tariffs.

**February 2026:** Modi's government signed a framework for an interim trade agreement with the US. As part of this, New Delhi committed to stop importing energy from Moscow and increase purchasing of American energy products. India — a sovereign nation — formally committed its energy purchasing decisions to Washington in a trade deal.

**Then the Iran war started.** In late February 2026, military action in the Middle East halted traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, which accounts for about 40 percent of India's oil imports. Russian oil tankers were now stranded off India's coast. India needed that oil desperately.

So what did India do? India approached President Donald Trump's approval to acquire several shipments of crude oil from Russia as part of its response to the ongoing conflict.

Read that again slowly. **India asked America for permission to buy oil.** Not "we are buying oil because it is our sovereign right." Not "we determine our own energy policy." India — a nuclear power, a civilization 5,000 years old, the world's fifth largest economy — *asked Trump for permission.*

The US then issued a 30-day waiver to New Delhi for purchasing crude from Moscow as the Iran war upends global supplies. Thirty days. After which India must come back and ask again.

Treasury Secretary Bessent called India an "essential partner" of the United States and said he "fully anticipates that New Delhi will ramp up purchases of US oil" once the crisis passes.

In other words: here is your 30-day permission slip. Now remember who you owe.

One imagines Indira Gandhi watching from wherever great leaders go, quietly putting her head in her hands.

The comic doubt that every thinking Indian is entitled to ask openly: **Is he a puppet?**

Not as an insult. As a genuine, evidence-based political question. With receipts.

## India Has Had Gods and Warriors as Leaders. And Some Who Were Both.

History is not kind to the idea that only men can lead with courage. India's own record destroys that idea completely.

**Indira Gandhi** — already discussed. Made Nixon blink.

**Sarojini Naidu** — the Nightingale of India, first woman President of the Indian National Congress, fearless voice in the independence movement. Called the British Empire what it was — to their faces.

**Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi** — took up arms against the British East India Company in 1857 when the men around her were calculating their surrender. Died in battle, sword in hand, at approximately 29 years of age.

**B.R. Ambedkar** — Dalit son of a soldier who became the architect of the Indian Constitution, who challenged both British colonialism and upper-caste Hindu society simultaneously. Never compromised. Never surrendered his analysis to convenience.

**Subhas Chandra Bose** — raised an entire army abroad to fight the British when Gandhi's path felt too slow. Agreed with or not, the man had no interest in anyone's permission.

These people — men and women, Hindu and Muslim, upper caste and Dalit, South and North — shared one quality: they understood that leadership means standing for something even when it costs you something.

What has Modi's leadership cost him personally? His relationship with Adani and Ambani seems intact. His political power seems intact. His relationship with Washington and Tel Aviv seems intact.

Leadership without cost is called management. Sometimes it is called something else.

## Sovereignty Is Not a Campaign Slogan. It Is a Daily Practice.

*"Vishwaguru,"* they tell us. India as the world's teacher.

A world teacher that takes its oil policy in 30-day installments from Washington.
A world teacher that goes quiet when Gaza burns.
A world teacher whose Prime Minister has not once, in ten years, said anything to America that America didn't want to hear.

Sovereignty is not achieved by changing the name of cities, renaming railway stations, or putting the Prime Minister's face on government advertisements. Sovereignty is the daily practice of making your own decisions — about your energy, your alliances, your voice in the world — based on your own interests and your own values.

India has the size, the economy, the nuclear capability, the civilizational depth, and the democratic legitimacy to be genuinely non-aligned and genuinely powerful. We do not need to choose between Washington and Beijing. We do not need to perform friendship with Netanyahu while our Muslim citizens watch. We do not need to accept 30-day oil permits from a country that was, within living memory, sanctioning us.

We had leaders who knew this. We had a woman Prime Minister who made the United States Seventh Fleet turn around in the Indian Ocean.

What we have now is a government that has mistaken loudness for strength, nationalism for sovereignty, and proximity to powerful foreign leaders for international respect.

## Who Should Lead India?

This is the real question — and it deserves a real answer, not a partisan one.

India does not need a Prime Minister who went to a famous university abroad (though education matters enormously). It needs a Prime Minister who has genuinely read the history — India's history, the world's history, the history of what happens to countries that outsource their foreign policy.

It needs someone who can sit across from an American President and say, as Indira Gandhi effectively said: *your aircraft carrier is not welcome in our ocean.*

It needs someone who understands that the question of Palestine is not a Muslim question or a Hindu question but a justice question — and that India, as a former colony, has a particular moral obligation and a particular moral authority to speak clearly on justice.

It needs someone who is not for sale to the RSS, to Washington, or to Tel Aviv — not because those relationships don't matter strategically, but because when they conflict with human dignity and international law, human dignity must win.

A chaiwala could be that person. A professor could be that person. A farmer's daughter could be that person. Lakshmibai was a queen but she fought on horseback.

The qualification is not the job title you held before. The qualification is whether you have a spine — and whether you are willing to use it when it matters.

India is still waiting.

## The AI Race: India Brought a Bicycle to a Formula 1 Track

Let us talk about Artificial Intelligence — the technology that will define the next 50 years of human civilization.

Here is what the world is investing in AI, in numbers that every Indian citizen deserves to know:

- **China**: invested approximately ¥890 billion ($125 billion) in AI in 2025 alone — government leading at $56 billion, with tech giants Alibaba and Tencent pouring in hundreds of billions more.
- **Japan**: committed at least 10 trillion yen — about $65 billion — through fiscal year 2030 to boost the semiconductor and AI industries, and that's just the government's share.
- **United States**: planning to deploy up to $500 billion through the Stargate Project over four years. Microsoft alone committed $17.5 billion *just for India's cloud infrastructure*.

And India? The Union Cabinet approved the IndiaAI Mission with a budget outlay of ₹10,372 crore ($1.2 billion) spread over five years.

Five years. $1.2 billion total. While China spends $125 billion in a single year.

To put this in terms even a RSS shakha graduate can understand: China is spending roughly **100 times more** on AI than India's entire five-year plan — every single year.

Japan's government AI budget for one fiscal year is larger than India's entire five-year commitment. The United States is spending more in a single month on AI infrastructure than India has committed for half a decade.

This is the country that Modi says will be "Vishwaguru" — the world's teacher. Teaching the world what, exactly? How to budget?

The truly comic part: while Modi was visiting Israel's parliament in February 2026 — giving speeches and creating viral moments — China was deploying AI systems in hospitals, farms, factories, and classrooms across a nation of 1.4 billion people. Japan was quadrupling its AI budget. The US was building hyperscale data centers. And India was proudly announcing that it now has 38,000 GPUs.

China has **230 AI data centre clusters**. India currently has 8.

Eight. We have eight. The country that invented zero, and we are using it frequently to describe our AI infrastructure.

## Late to the Party: India's Government Discovered AI After Everyone Else Did

Here is a timeline that every Indian voter deserves to see clearly:

- **2017** — The transformer architecture is published. This is the foundational paper behind every modern AI system — ChatGPT, Gemini, DeepSeek, all of it. The entire world's AI race starts here.
- **2018** — NITI Aayog publishes India's first National AI Strategy. On paper, good. In practice, it focused on narrow sector applications — agriculture, healthcare — rather than building foundational AI infrastructure.
- **2018** — China launches its national AI development plan, backed by hundreds of billions in state investment. The EU begins its AI Act framework. The US starts pouring federal money into AI research.
- **2020** — OpenAI releases GPT-3. The era of large language models begins. The writing is on the wall for anyone paying attention.
- **2022** — ChatGPT launches. It becomes the fastest-growing consumer application in history. Every government in the world wakes up — if they hadn't already.
- **March 2024** — India finally launches the IndiaAI Mission. The mission launched two months after China's AI chatbot DeepSeek-R1 was released — whether a knee-jerk reaction or a well-thought-out strategy, it's hard to tell.

Seven years after the transformer paper. Six years after China started its national AI plan. Nearly two years after ChatGPT made it undeniable to even the most distracted government on earth.

India launched its national AI mission in 2024. By this point, GPT-3 had already been released in 2020, BERT in 2018, transformers in 2017 — the paradigm had already shifted and India was playing catch-up.

And what has the mission delivered? More than a year after launch, experts say none of the IndiaAI Mission programs have shown results — no models have been produced from GPU grants, and almost all grants are purely computational.

India took 21 months to disburse just 31% of the FY24-25 budget. China iterated four models in 12 months. India produced zero government-funded competitive foundation models in the same period.

The most painful part: India accounts for just 1.4% of publications at top AI conferences, ranks 14th globally, and has produced virtually zero research on foundation model training or architectural innovations. Meanwhile, despite ranking second globally in AI skill penetration, India experiences net negative migration — top researchers are concentrated at OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta, and Anthropic — representing an estimated annual talent value loss of $150–500 million.

India is training the world's AI researchers — and then the world's AI companies are hiring them, because the Indian government was too busy renaming railway stations to build the infrastructure that would keep them here.

The comic summary: while Modi was taking selfies with Netanyahu in Israel and accepting 30-day oil permits from Washington, China was building 230 AI data centre clusters and iterating foundation models quarterly. India built 8 clusters and is still having meetings about the strategy.

*Vishwaguru.* The world's teacher. Teaching the world, apparently, how to be six years late to the most important technological race of the 21st century.

 — And India Is... What Exactly?

On February 25, 2026, Narendra Modi became the first Indian Prime Minister to address Israel's parliament, the Knesset. While Gaza was still burning. While the bodies of 165 children from the Minab school were still being counted.

During his speech, a clip went viral across India and internationally. Modi was quoted saying "India is the Motherland, Israel is the Fatherland" — sparking immediate outrage.

Now, to be fair — and this article believes in being fair even when it is inconvenient — fact-checkers confirmed the clip was taken out of context. Modi was actually speaking about the Indian-origin Jewish community living in Israel, saying they consider Israel their fatherland and India their motherland.

Fair enough. Context matters.

But here is what *cannot* be taken out of context:

The Indian Prime Minister chose to visit Israel's parliament — for the first time in history — *while Israel was engaged in an active military operation that had killed over 165 children in a school bombing just days before.* He did not visit Gaza. He did not call for a ceasefire. He did not mention the school in Minab. He spoke about cooperation, partnership, and shared values.

The reaction from Indians was swift. "India looks submissive, diplomacy childish and the whole world is laughing at us," wrote one widely-shared commentator. Another asked: "How could he equate Israel with India? Israel may be father to Israelis and bhakts but not for Indians."

And someone on social media summarized what many thinking Indians felt: *"इसलिए भारत को अब एक पढ़े-लिखे PM की ज़रूरत हैं"* — "That is why India now needs an educated PM."

For once, social media got it exactly right.

We did not survive all of that — thousands of years of it — to become a country that takes its foreign policy in 30-day installments.

Our sovereignty is not a gift from America or Israel or anyone else. It was bought with the lives of people who fought for it — Lakshmibai, Bhagat Singh, Ambedkar, Gandhi, Nehru, and yes, Indira Gandhi who stared down a nuclear carrier.

We owe them, at minimum, the courage to ask: *who is actually running this country?*

And if the answer makes us uncomfortable — good. Discomfort is where thinking begins.
