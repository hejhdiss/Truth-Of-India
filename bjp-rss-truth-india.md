## Chapter 1: What RSS Actually Is — Not What They Tell You

The Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh was founded in 1925 by K.B. Hedgewar. Its second and most influential leader, M.S. Golwalkar, wrote the foundational ideological texts that still guide the organization.

In *We, or Our Nationhood Defined* (1939), Golwalkar wrote admiringly of Nazi Germany's racial purification policies as a model for how India should deal with its minorities. He wrote that non-Hindus in India should live "subordinate to the Hindu nation, claiming nothing, deserving no privileges, far less any preferential treatment — not even citizen's rights."

This is not a misquote. This is not taken out of context. The full text is publicly available. Indian historians, international scholars, and India's own Supreme Court have all cited it.

RSS was banned three times by the Indian government — after Gandhi's assassination in 1948 (a former RSS member, Nathuram Godse, killed Gandhi), after the Emergency in 1975, and after the Babri Masjid demolition in 1992.

BJP is the political arm of RSS. Every BJP Prime Minister — Vajpayee, Modi — came from RSS. Every major BJP policy decision is filtered through RSS's ideological framework. This is not a conspiracy theory. BJP leaders say this openly and proudly.

Understanding BJP requires understanding RSS first. They are not separate entities. They are the same project.

## Chapter 2: Democracy Under Attack — The Institutions That Were Broken

### The Election Commission

India's Election Commission was once considered one of the most credible electoral bodies in the world. Under Modi's tenure, it has been systematically weakened.

In 2023, the Supreme Court of India struck down the government's method of appointing Election Commissioners — which had removed the Chief Justice of India from the selection committee — calling it unconstitutional and warning that it created conditions for partisan appointments.

In the 2024 general elections, opposition parties filed over 1,000 complaints with the Election Commission about BJP's use of government machinery for campaign purposes. The Commission took action on virtually none of them.

Former Chief Election Commissioner S.Y. Quraishi and multiple retired IAS officers publicly stated that the institutional independence of the Commission had been compromised under BJP's tenure.

### The Central Bureau of Investigation

The CBI — India's premier investigative agency — has been called a "caged parrot" by the Supreme Court of India itself as far back as 2013. Under BJP, this characterization became more accurate, not less.

Opposition leaders across parties — Congress, Trinamool Congress, AAP, NCP, Shiv Sena (Uddhav faction) — were investigated by the CBI and Enforcement Directorate immediately after they refused to join BJP or after they contested BJP in elections.

The pattern is documented: Hemant Soren (Jharkhand CM, arrested), Arvind Kejriwal (Delhi CM, arrested during election campaign), Manish Sisodia (Delhi Deputy CM, held in custody for 17 months before bail), Satyendar Jain (Delhi Minister, jailed), K. Chandrashekhar Rao (investigated after losing Telangana election to Congress).

Notably: several politicians who were under active CBI/ED investigation dropped their cases entirely after joining BJP. This pattern — documented by The Wire, The Hindu, and Scroll.in — has been called "washing machine politics" by opposition leaders and independent journalists alike.

### The Judiciary

India's Supreme Court — once a genuine check on executive power — has faced serious credibility questions under BJP's tenure.

In January 2018, four sitting Supreme Court judges — including the senior-most judge — held an unprecedented press conference stating that the Chief Justice was selectively assigning sensitive cases to "benches of preference" to ensure favourable outcomes for the government.

The court took over four years to hear petitions challenging the abrogation of Article 370 in Kashmir — an abrogation that the government's own legal team acknowledged had no clear constitutional precedent.

The court has repeatedly declined to hear or delayed hearing petitions on electoral bonds (a BJP fundraising scheme later struck down as unconstitutional), the sedition law, the Citizenship Amendment Act, and the detention of activists and journalists under the UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act).

Former Chief Justice of India D.Y. Chandrachud, in his farewell speech in 2024, acknowledged that the court had not always lived up to its constitutional responsibilities.

## Chapter 3: Press Freedom — How India Went From 140th to 159th

In 2014, when Modi came to power, India ranked 140th on the Reporters Without Borders World Press Freedom Index.

By 2023, India had fallen to **161st out of 180 countries** — below Pakistan, below Afghanistan under the Taliban, below every functioning democracy in Asia.

By 2025, India ranked **151st** — a marginal recovery, but still one of the worst rankings for any democracy of India's size.

What happened in between:

**Journalists arrested under UAPA:** The UAPA — originally an anti-terrorism law — has been used against journalists, academics, and activists with increasing frequency. Those arrested include Siddique Kappan (journalist arrested while travelling to cover the Hathras rape case, held for two years), Fahad Shah (Kashmir editor, arrested multiple times), and Rupesh Kumar Singh (freelance journalist, arrested without charges for over a year).

**NewsClick raids:** In October 2023, Delhi Police conducted simultaneous raids on over 46 journalists associated with NewsClick, a digital media outlet. Founder Prabir Purkayastha was arrested under UAPA. The Supreme Court later granted bail, calling the arrest procedurally illegal — but the message to independent media was clear.

**Seizure of BBC offices:** In February 2023, the Income Tax department conducted a three-day "survey" of BBC India's offices in Delhi and Mumbai, days after BBC broadcast a documentary critical of Modi's role in the 2002 Gujarat riots. The documentary had already been banned on YouTube and Twitter in India under emergency IT rules.

**The Wire harassment:** The Wire, one of India's most credible independent outlets, faced repeated legal harassment, advertiser pressure, and government intimidation. Its founders and editors have been targeted with multiple defamation and criminal cases.

**Ownership concentration:** By 2024, a significant majority of India's mainstream television news channels were owned by businesspeople with close ties to BJP or the Modi government. Mukesh Ambani's Reliance holds stakes in Network18, which owns CNN-News18, CNBC-TV18, News18 India, and dozens of regional channels. The conflict of interest is structural, not incidental.

## Chapter 4: The Economy — What The Numbers Actually Say

BJP came to power in 2014 promising **"acche din"** — good days. Specifically: 2 crore jobs per year, doubling of farmer incomes, controlling inflation, and GDP growth above 8%.

Twelve years later, the record:

**Unemployment:** India's unemployment rate among educated youth (graduates aged 15-29) reached **42.3%** in 2023-24, according to the Centre for Monitoring Indian Economy (CMIE) — the highest since data collection began. India needs to create approximately 12 million new jobs per year just to absorb new entrants into the workforce. It has consistently failed to do so.

**Demonetisation (November 2016):** Modi announced on live television with four hours' notice that 86% of India's currency — ₹500 and ₹1,000 notes — would be invalid from midnight. The stated goal was to eliminate black money, stop counterfeit currency, and transition India to a cashless economy.

The results, documented by the Reserve Bank of India itself: 99.3% of the demonetised currency was returned to banks, meaning virtually no black money was destroyed. The informal economy — which employs over 90% of India's workers — contracted sharply. Over 100 people died standing in queues outside banks. GDP growth fell from 8% to 6.1% in the following quarter. The RBI's own annual report confirmed the policy achieved none of its stated objectives.

**Electoral Bonds:** In 2018, BJP introduced the Electoral Bond scheme — anonymous political funding through bonds purchased from the State Bank of India. Opposition parties and transparency advocates immediately challenged it as a legalized corruption mechanism.

In February 2024, the Supreme Court of India struck down Electoral Bonds as unconstitutional, calling them a violation of the right to information and noting they created a "quid pro quo" mechanism between corporate donors and the ruling party. The court ordered full disclosure of donor data.

The disclosed data revealed: companies that had received government contracts, tax raids, or regulatory action donated billions to BJP in the months surrounding those events. The correlation between government pressure and donations was documented by multiple Indian news organizations.

**Adani:** The relationship between BJP and the Adani Group — led by Gautam Adani, Modi's close associate from Gujarat — has been examined by the Hindenburg Research report (January 2023), which alleged stock manipulation, accounting fraud, and improper use of offshore entities. The Adani Group lost $100 billion in market value within days.

The Supreme Court appointed a committee to investigate. The committee's report was widely criticized as inadequate. No regulatory action has been taken. Adani's businesses continued to receive major government contracts — in airports, ports, defence, and green energy — throughout the controversy.

## Chapter 5: Minorities — The Documented Record

### Muslims

India has the world's third largest Muslim population — approximately 200 million people. Under BJP, their legal status, physical safety, and economic participation have all deteriorated measurably.

**Citizenship Amendment Act (2020):** The CAA created a pathway to Indian citizenship for refugees from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan — but explicitly excluded Muslims. This was the first time in independent India's history that religion was used as a criterion for citizenship. The UN Human Rights Office called it "fundamentally discriminatory." Mass protests erupted across India.

**NRC (National Register of Citizens):** In Assam, the NRC exercise left approximately 1.9 million people — including many Hindus — without citizenship documentation. The government's stated intention to extend NRC nationally has created a climate of fear among Muslims across India who may lack documentation for historical reasons unrelated to immigration status.

**Demolitions:** The practice of demolishing the homes and properties of Muslim accused persons — before any conviction, purely on the basis of accusation — became so widespread under BJP state governments that the Supreme Court of India was forced to intervene in 2024, calling it illegal and unconstitutional. The practice had been nicknamed "bulldozer justice" and celebrated openly by BJP politicians including Uttar Pradesh CM Yogi Adityanath.

**Mob lynching:** Between 2014 and 2024, India saw a documented surge in mob violence — primarily targeting Muslims and Dalits, often over accusations of cow slaughter or beef consumption. India Spend, an independent data journalism outlet, documented over 100 deaths in cow-related mob violence between 2010 and 2019, with the overwhelming majority occurring after 2014.

**Hijab ban:** In 2022, Karnataka's BJP government banned the hijab in educational institutions. The Karnataka High Court upheld the ban. Several Muslim girls were forced to choose between their education and their religion.

### Christians

Christian communities — primarily in Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Manipur, and Uttar Pradesh — have documented increasing incidents of church vandalism, forced "ghar wapsi" (re-conversion) ceremonies, and anti-conversion laws that are selectively applied against Christian missionaries.

In 2023, ethnic violence in Manipur — between Meitei and Kuki communities — included the burning of over 250 churches. The Modi government took over 77 days to make a public statement. When he finally spoke, it was after a video of two Kuki women being paraded naked by a Meitei mob went viral internationally.

### Dalits

The Hathras gang rape case (2020) — in which a 19-year-old Dalit woman was gang raped, her body cremated by police at 2 AM without family consent, and journalists attempting to cover it were arrested under UAPA — exposed the intersection of caste violence and BJP governance in Uttar Pradesh.

Atrocities against Dalits have risen consistently under BJP governance. According to India's own National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), crimes against Scheduled Castes increased by over 45% between 2015 and 2022.

## Chapter 6: Kashmir — What Actually Happened

On August 5, 2019, the Modi government abrogated Article 370 of the Indian Constitution — which gave Jammu & Kashmir special autonomous status — through a procedure that multiple constitutional lawyers called illegal.

What followed:

- The entire Kashmir Valley was placed under military lockdown
- All communications — phones, internet, newspapers — were cut for months
- Over 5,000 people were detained, including three former Chief Ministers
- The internet shutdown in Kashmir became the longest ever imposed in a democracy, lasting over 18 months in various forms

The Supreme Court took four years to rule on petitions challenging the abrogation — and when it did in December 2023, it upheld the government's action while calling for elections to be restored.

Human rights organizations including Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch documented extensive abuses during the lockdown period — arbitrary detention, torture, denial of medical care — all of which the government denied.

Since 2019, Kashmiri Pandits who had returned to the Valley under government resettlement schemes have been targeted in a series of targeted killings. The government has consistently failed to protect them. Their situation — used as a political argument to justify the abrogation — has not materially improved.

## Chapter 7: Education — Rewriting India's Mind

The systematic revision of NCERT textbooks under BJP has been documented exhaustively by The Wire, Indian Express, and The Hindu. Key deletions include:

- **The Mughal contribution to Indian culture and architecture** — reduced to minimal references
- **The RSS's non-participation in the independence movement** — references removed
- **The ideological background of Gandhi's assassin** — removed from political science texts
- **The Emergency (1975)** — context and analysis reduced
- **Evolution** — chapters on Darwin's theory of evolution removed from Class 10 science textbooks in 2023
- **Periodic table** — portions removed from Class 11 chemistry
- **Democracy and diversity** — chapter removed from Class 10 political science

In 2023, the National Council of Educational Research and Training (NCERT) Director Dinesh Prasad Saklani defended the deletions by saying the content was "outdated" and that students were "overburdened." Independent educators and scientists uniformly rejected this justification.

The Indian Science Congress — the country's premier science body — has repeatedly had to distance itself from pseudoscientific claims made by BJP ministers and ideologues, including claims that ancient Indians invented aeroplanes, stem cell research, and internet technology.

But taken together — the pattern is clear:
