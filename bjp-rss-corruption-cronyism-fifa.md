# The Corruption Machine: How the BJP-RSS Government Looted India, Built Billionaires, and Wrecked Its Football

*A Critical Investigative Report | Sources: Al Jazeera, PBS NewsHour, Nature/Humanities and Social Sciences Communications, The Reporters' Collective, The Wire, GIJN (Global Investigative Journalism Network), Inside World Football, ESPN, MR Online, Wikipedia (Scandals in India), India Legal, Field Vision*

---

## Introduction

When Narendra Modi swept to power in 2014, he campaigned on a single, thunderous promise: ending corruption. The slogan *"Na khaunga, na khane dunga"* — "I will not take bribes, nor allow others to" — became the rallying cry of the BJP's electoral machine. Eleven years later, the record tells a very different story.

What has emerged under BJP and RSS rule is not just ordinary political corruption, but a sophisticated system in which state power, corporate wealth, law enforcement agencies, and electoral financing have been fused into a machine that serves an elite few at the expense of India's 1.4 billion people. The evidence for this system comes not from opposition pamphlets, but from India's own Supreme Court, the United States Department of Justice, peer-reviewed academic research, and investigations by some of India's most decorated journalists.

This article examines three interlocking dimensions of this crisis: the electoral bonds scandal and weaponisation of law enforcement; the documented cronyism benefiting Adani Group and Reliance; and the governance collapse in Indian football that culminated in a historic FIFA ban.

## Part 1: The Electoral Bonds Scandal — Institutionalised Corruption at Scale

### How the Scheme Worked

In 2018, the BJP government introduced the Electoral Bonds Scheme — sold to the public as a transparency reform to clean up political financing. In reality, it operated as the opposite.

Under the scheme, corporations could purchase bearer bonds from the State Bank of India (India's government-owned bank) and donate them to political parties anonymously — meaning the public could never know who was funding whom. There was no limit on the amount. The funds left no public trace. Yet — and this is the critical detail exposed by investigative journalist Poonam Agarwal — the government was secretly tracking every donor through a hidden unique alphanumeric code embedded in each bond. The public was kept in the dark. The ruling party was not.

On February 15, 2024, a five-judge Constitutional Bench of India's Supreme Court struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme unanimously as **unconstitutional**. The Court found that the scheme violated the right to information, and that it was based on illegal amendments to multiple acts of Parliament. It ordered the State Bank of India to release all data on donors and recipients.

### The Data: A Map of Institutionalised Quid Pro Quo

When the data was released, the scale of what had been happening became clear. According to investigations by Al Jazeera, The Wire, and a peer-reviewed paper published in *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications* (Nature Publishing Group), the BJP received approximately **Rs 6,060.5 crore (US$726 million)** through electoral bonds — about 93% of the total money flowing through the scheme. The next largest recipient, Trinamool Congress, received less than a quarter of that sum.

More importantly, the data revealed a pattern of systematic quid pro quo. The peer-reviewed research identified four distinct forms of corruption in the data:

**"Chanda do, dhanda lo"** (Give donation, get business): Companies with no track record or revenue suddenly reporting income surges and immediately donating large sums to the BJP. Entities linked to Reliance affiliates — Honeywell Properties and Chander Commercials — reportedly diverted their entire revenues of Rs 50 crore each to the BJP in FY 2021-22, raising immediate questions about the origin of their wealth.

**"Hafta-vasuli"** (Extortion): Perhaps the most damning pattern. Multiple companies under investigation by central enforcement agencies donated hundreds of crores to the BJP — and their investigations subsequently stalled or were quietly dropped. Take Aurobindo Pharma: its executive Sarath Chandra Reddy was arrested for bribery. While under investigation, the company bought electoral bonds that went entirely to the BJP. Seven months later, Reddy became a state witness. Then, in November 2023, the company donated another Rs 250 million to the BJP. His son subsequently joined the party.

The single largest buyer in the entire dataset was Future Gaming and Hotel Services, run by "lottery king" Santiago Martin, who had been under money laundering investigation since 2007 and whose assets were attached by the Enforcement Directorate in 2022. Despite this, the company purchased bonds worth Rs 1,368 crore between 2020 and 2024. Since the bond purchase period began, the ED's investigation appears to have stalled. Martin's son has joined the BJP.

**"Theka lo, rishwat do"** (Bag contract, give bribe): Implicit agreements between corporations and the ruling party — donations in exchange for government contracts.

Transparency campaigner Commodore (Retired) Lokesh Batra, who petitioned the Supreme Court for disclosure, described the dataset as "reeling with evidence suggesting quid pro quo understanding."

### The Washing Machine: How the BJP Laundered Politicians' Corruption Charges

The Electoral Bonds scandal was not the only mechanism. The Wire reported on a parallel, equally troubling pattern: **25 politicians who were facing corruption investigations by central agencies crossed over to the BJP after 2014. Of these, 23 received relief in their cases** — three cases closed entirely, twenty others frozen in place.

The Indian Express investigation documented the political leaders involved: Ajit Pawar, Praful Patel, Ashok Chavan, Himanta Biswa Sarma, Suvendu Adhikari, and others — politicians from Congress, NCP, TMC, and Shiv Sena who had been under serious investigation before defecting to the BJP and subsequently watching their cases go cold.

A Scroll investigation found that 95% of Enforcement Directorate cases since 2014 were filed against Opposition leaders. Between 2014 and September 2022, 115 of 121 prominent leaders who came under ED scrutiny were from opposition parties. The Wire described the ED as having become an "Excessive Directorate." This is the enforcement of law not as justice, but as political weapon — used to intimidate opponents while shielding allies.

## Part 2: The Adani-Modi Nexus — Cronyism as Government Policy

No relationship better illustrates the BJP government's corruption than its documented ties to the Adani Group — a business empire that, under Modi's watch, grew from a mid-sized Gujarat conglomerate into India's largest corporation, worth $206 billion at its peak.

### The Gujarat Connection

The Adani Group's relationship with Modi predates his national premiership. The foundations were laid during his tenure as Chief Minister of Gujarat, during which Adani's empire expanded dramatically in the state — ports, power, gas infrastructure — on terms critics described as uniquely favourable.

When Modi won the 2014 national election, he flew to Delhi on an Adani Group jet. In 2024, his campaign was once again documented as using Adani aircraft. In the ten years between those flights, the Adani Group's transformation under BJP national governance was extraordinary.

### Airport Capture

In February 2019, the Modi government invited private bids to manage six airports. The Adani Group — which had zero prior experience in airport management — bid against established players like GMR, which had managed airports for years. Adani won all six contracts simultaneously. No single company had previously won such a sweep of airport privatisation bids in Indian history. Scroll.in reported on this remarkable outcome and the unanswered questions around how a company with no track record defeated specialists across every single bid.

### Rigged Power Tenders

The Reporters' Collective — whose lead journalist Poonam Agarwal has won the BBC News Award and Ramnath Goenka Awards — has documented in forensic detail how the BJP government scripted solar power auctions to benefit the Adani Group.

In 2019, the Solar Energy Corporation of India (SECI), under the Ministry of New and Renewable Energy, structured a "manufacturing-linked auction" with extraordinary terms: a bidder would need to be capable of supplying power generated from both solar and thermal sources simultaneously. This unusual twin-source requirement excluded almost all competitors. The result was a tender with only three bidders — Adani Group, Azure Power, and Navayuga Engineering — and only Adani Group was genuinely positioned to win. Navayuga bid the highest permitted tariff and lost. It then immediately sold its Andhra Pradesh port to the Adani Group for Rs 3,375 crore — a deal that had been under negotiation throughout the bidding process, raising serious collusion questions that the government never investigated.

The Reporters' Collective also documented how BJP-ruled Maharashtra and Rajasthan issued strikingly similar tenders for 25-year electricity supply contracts with the same unusual twin-source criteria — effectively custom-built for Adani Group's business plans. One of these tenders was withdrawn after the Collective published its investigation.

### The U.S. Indictment

In November 2024, the United States Department of Justice and the Securities and Exchange Commission filed criminal and civil charges against Gautam Adani, his nephew Sagar Adani, and six associates. The indictment, filed in a New York federal court, alleged that:

- Adani officials orchestrated a scheme to pay approximately **$265 million in bribes** to Indian government officials to secure solar energy contracts
- These contracts, once secured, would generate an estimated **$2 billion in profit** over 20 years
- The group **raised $175 million from American investors** while concealing the bribery scheme from them, constituting securities fraud under U.S. law

The consequences were immediate and international. Kenya's President William Ruto cancelled a $736 million deal with Adani Group — including a 30-year airport concession and a power transmission project — saying in his State of the Nation address that "in the face of undisputed evidence or credible information on corruption, I will not hesitate to take decisive action." Bangladesh's courts ordered a separate inquiry into Adani's energy contracts there. TotalEnergies of France announced a halt to all new investments. Credit ratings agency S&P Global revised its outlook on multiple Adani businesses to negative.

The Modi government's response to the indictment was silence. The BJP's IT Cell head Amit Malviya said the charges were "allegations" and the defendants "presumed innocent" — which critics read as an official show of support for Adani. Parliament was disrupted by opposition demands for debate, which the ruling coalition blocked. No Indian investigation was ordered.

Independent market analyst Ambareesh Baliga told PBS NewsHour that investors already know "just how ingrained this (bribes and corruption) is in the fabric of the Indian economy."

### The Reliance Connection

Reliance Industries, owned by Mukesh Ambani, has shown its own documented links to BJP financing. The peer-reviewed research in *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications* flagged entities linked to Reliance affiliates funnelling revenues directly to the BJP through electoral bonds. The company has positioned itself as a major beneficiary of government telecommunications policy, with Reliance Jio's rise facilitated by regulatory decisions that critics argued systematically disadvantaged competitors — a charge Reliance denies.

## Part 3: How Political Corruption Destroyed Indian Football and Triggered a FIFA Ban

India, a nation of 1.4 billion people, has never once qualified for the FIFA World Cup. In August 2022, it achieved the extraordinary humiliation of being suspended by FIFA entirely — stripping the country of the right to host the U-17 Women's World Cup with fewer than 60 days to go, and banning every Indian national team from international competition.

The cause was political corruption in the All India Football Federation (AIFF). And the BJP's fingerprints are all over it.

### The Praful Patel Era: A Politician Who Refused to Leave

The groundwork for the 2022 crisis was laid by Praful Patel, a politician (initially Congress, later Nationalist Congress Party), who became AIFF president in 2012 and refused to vacate the position even after his term expired. By 2022, India's Supreme Court — concerned that the AIFF had been essentially captured by Patel and was operating in violation of its own constitution — appointed a Committee of Administrators (CoA) to run the federation and oversee elections.

FIFA, under its statutes, prohibits "third-party interference" — meaning any body other than the elected football association cannot run federation affairs. It does not matter if that third party is a court. When the Supreme Court's CoA began making football governance decisions, FIFA issued warnings, then ultimatums, and on August 16, 2022, suspended the AIFF outright.

The official FIFA statement said the suspension was due to "undue influence from third parties, which constitutes a serious violation of the FIFA Statutes." India immediately lost hosting rights for the Women's U-17 World Cup. Every Indian team was banned from international competition.

The suspension was lifted within weeks once elections were held and the CoA stood down. But the damage to Indian football's international standing, finances, and development pipeline was significant.

### BJP's Man Takes the Helm — And Makes It Worse

In the elections that followed the suspension, Kalyan Chaubey — a BJP party member — defeated football legend Bhaichung Bhutia to become AIFF president. India Legal, an independent legal journal, noted pointedly that Chaubey "is not just a former footballer, but also an active member of the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party."

The concern, which has since been validated by events, was that political control of Indian football would simply continue under a new party colour. AIFF Secretary General Shaji Prabhakaran, who served under Chaubey before being dismissed in early 2024, filed a formal ethics complaint against the president with AIFF's ethics committee. In his complaint, documented by Inside World Football, he accused Chaubey of:

- A conflict of interest
- A bribery attempt
- Misrepresentation and misinformation
- Violations of the AIFF constitution
- Actions that "damaged the brand values of AIFF" and "dented the negotiation power of AIFF with its marketing partner"
- Resulting in investors losing confidence in Indian football

Prabhakaran's letter directly attributed India's disastrous footballing record to the administration: "The Indian men's team didn't win a single match in the calendar year 2024. It is the direct result of his actions."

India's FIFA ranking is now among the worst of the last decade. The men's team is trapped in a governance crisis that continues to paralyse the sport. Senior players, including legendary captain Sunil Chhetri, have made public appeals for FIFA to intervene again. Field Vision, a sports analysis publication, reported that as of early 2026, "domestic tournaments have been brought to a stop and the players themselves are not sure of their career prospects."

### The Pattern: Political Figures Across Indian Sport

The AIFF crisis is not an isolated case. It reflects a broader disease of Indian sports governance: political figures from the BJP and other parties treat sports federations as fiefdoms.

Brij Bhushan Sharan Singh, a BJP Member of Parliament, headed the Wrestling Federation of India (WFI) for years. In 2023, India's most celebrated wrestlers — Olympic and Commonwealth Games medallists — staged a public protest at Jantar Mantar in New Delhi, accusing Singh of **sexual harassment of multiple women wrestlers**. The protest became a national scandal. Singh was eventually forced to step down from the federation presidency — but faced no immediate criminal consequences, and the BJP initially defended him, with multiple party officials appearing alongside him at public events during the protest period.

India Legal concluded: "Sports governance in India has long been dominated by political heavyweights... Their influence often outlasts their political careers, creating dynastic control within sports administration."

## Conclusion: The Price of the Corruption Machine

What connects these three areas — electoral corruption, Adani cronyism, and sports governance — is a single structural reality: under the BJP and with RSS ideological backing, state power has been systematically privatised and weaponised for the benefit of a connected elite.

The electoral bonds system let corporations pay the government for regulatory favours — and walk free from investigation if they paid enough. The Adani Group received airports, ports, and solar contracts through processes that investigators and international prosecutors have described as rigged or corrupt. Indian football was handed to a political operative while India's wrestlers had to beg for protection from their own federation president on the streets of Delhi.

The 95% statistic on ED cases against opposition politicians says it all: India's anti-corruption machinery has not been dismantled under the BJP. It has been captured and redirected — not to fight corruption, but to protect the corrupt and punish their critics.

For ordinary Indians — who pay more for gas, eat more expensive food, work in an economy where FDI has collapsed by 96.5% and startup funding has plunged — the cost of this system is paid every day, at every meal, with every bill.

The BJP came to power promising *"Na khaunga, na khane dunga."* The evidence accumulated since 2014 tells us: they were only half right about the second half.
