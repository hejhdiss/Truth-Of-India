# The Money Trail: How Modi's Government Built a Corruption Machine

## Adani, Electoral Bonds, and the ₹10,000 Crore Black Hole Nobody Is Allowed to Ask About

*By Muhammed Shafin P | Malappuram, Kerala*

*Sources: Hindenburg Research, OCCRP / The Guardian / Financial Times joint investigation, U.S. Department of Justice, U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, Supreme Court of India, Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR), The Wire, Scroll.in, The Federal, The News Minute, Wikipedia (PM CARES Fund), Deccan Herald, Al Jazeera*

---

Every government has corruption. What makes Modi's government different is the **architecture** — the deliberate construction of systems that make corruption invisible, unauditable, and legally untouchable.

Three structures define that architecture:

1. **The Adani relationship** — the biggest alleged corporate fraud in Indian history, with an American indictment, Swiss criminal investigation, and regulatory inquiry that was quietly buried.
2. **Electoral Bonds** — a political funding machine the Supreme Court of India unanimously struck down as unconstitutional and called a tool for "crony capitalism."
3. **PM CARES Fund** — over ₹10,000 crore collected from the public in the name of COVID relief, with no RTI, no CAG audit, and no Parliament allowed to ask questions about it.

This is not opposition allegation. Every fact in this article is sourced to court records, international investigative journalism, or the government's own data.

---

## Part 1: The Adani Story — "The Largest Con in Corporate History"

### How It Started

In January 2023, Hindenburg Research — an American financial investigations firm — published a 106-page report on the Adani Group. The headline was direct: Gautam Adani, the report said, was *"pulling the largest con in corporate history."*

The specific allegations:
- **Brazen stock manipulation** — Adani Group insiders secretly owned large blocks of publicly listed Adani stock through offshore shell companies in Mauritius, Cayman Islands, and other tax havens, in violation of Indian securities law requiring at least 25% of any listed company's stock to be publicly held
- **Accounting fraud** across multiple group companies
- **Massive undisclosed debt** hidden in offshore subsidiaries

The immediate result: Adani Group stocks crashed. The conglomerate suffered a major setback, with the Hindenburg report claiming it had spent decades engaged in "brazen stock manipulation" and "accounting fraud." Investors lost confidence overnight.

### What OCCRP Found — With the Guardian and Financial Times

Seven months later, the Organised Crime and Corruption Reporting Project (OCCRP) — the same investigative network that broke the Panama Papers — published its own investigation in partnership with The Guardian and the Financial Times.

OCCRP revealed that foreign associates of the Adanis secretly held large stakes in the family's business empire in possible violation of Indian law. Files from multiple tax havens, bank records, and internal Adani Group emails allowed reporters to identify two investors: Nasser Ali Shaban Ahli from the UAE and Chang Chung-Ling from Taiwan.

The two men invested hundreds of millions of dollars in publicly traded Adani stock through opaque investment funds based in Mauritius. These investments also appear to have been coordinated with an Adani company.

In 2017, the stocks held by these two individuals were valued at a whopping $430 million.

Why does this matter? Indian law forbids company insiders from owning more than 75% of a publicly listed company. Ahli and Chang have served as directors and shareholders in Adani Group companies and companies associated with Vinod Adani — Gautam Adani's brother. If they were holding stock secretly on behalf of the Adani family, the family's effective stake would breach the legal limit — meaning ordinary Indian retail investors were buying stock in a company that was manipulating its own share price.

### Then Came the American Indictment

In November 2024, the story escalated dramatically. U.S. federal prosecutors indicted the group's founder Gautam Adani and his nephew for allegedly promising to pay hundreds of millions of dollars in bribes to Indian officials. The Securities and Exchange Commission filed a parallel civil complaint, which is ongoing.

Let that land: the chairman of the Adani Group — the man described by international analysts as India's most powerful businessman and Modi's closest corporate ally — was criminally indicted in the United States for allegedly bribing Indian officials. The group has called the accusations "baseless."

### The Swiss Investigation

According to an August 2024 court ruling, prosecutors in Switzerland were conducting a criminal investigation into Chang, suspecting him of acting as a "front man" for Adani Group investments, and froze over $310 million in assets.

Three countries. Three separate investigations. American federal prosecutors, Swiss criminal investigators, and OCCRP's global investigative network all pointed at the same company.

### What Happened in India? Almost Nothing.

While battling criminal probes abroad, the Adani Group has faced only limited repercussions at home in India.

India's market regulator, SEBI, investigated. The Supreme Court had noted that Hindenburg and OCCRP reports could not be considered conclusive evidence, and found no infirmities in SEBI's probe. However, the Supreme Court-appointed expert committee itself found that SEBI had "hit a wall" because government agencies could not verify the identities of the suspicious investors due to convoluted offshore structures.

In other words: the offshore secrecy was so dense that India's own regulators could not see through it. SEBI has not published a comprehensive report on its inquiries into Adani Group, leaving many of the details outside public view.

Rahul Gandhi put the question directly at a press conference: "Why is there no investigation taking place?"

No answer came.

### The Modi-Adani Relationship

Opposition politicians allege that the firm has received preferential treatment from the government to secure its lucrative state contracts. Analysts describe its chairman, Gautam Adani, as benefiting from a cozy relationship with Modi.

This relationship is not secret. Modi and Adani have known each other since Modi was Chief Minister of Gujarat — Adani's home state. When Modi flew to Delhi to be sworn in as Prime Minister in 2014, he flew on Adani's private plane. The Adani Group's rise from a mid-sized Gujarat trading company to India's largest infrastructure conglomerate — controlling ports, airports, power plants, roads, and data centers — occurred during exactly the period Modi has been in power.

The Adani Group is perceived as close to Prime Minister Narendra Modi and is India's largest importer and private producer of coal. Under BJP governance, Adani has won government contracts for airports, ports, and infrastructure across the country at a scale no previous government allowed any single private group to achieve.

**This is the man facing criminal indictment in the United States. Modi has not commented.**

## Part 2: Electoral Bonds — The Supreme Court Called It "Crony Capitalism"

### What Electoral Bonds Were

In 2017, the BJP government introduced the Electoral Bond Scheme — presented as a way to "clean up" political funding by replacing cash donations with a formal financial instrument.

Here is how it worked: any company or individual could walk into a State Bank of India branch, buy a bond of any denomination, and donate it anonymously to any political party. The party could then cash the bond at SBI. No one — not the public, not the Election Commission, not the courts — could see who gave what to whom.

The BJP government passed the scheme as a Money Bill — bypassing the Rajya Sabha (upper house of Parliament) where it did not have a majority. It simultaneously amended the Companies Act to **remove the limit** on how much a company could donate to a political party. Previously, companies could donate a maximum of 7.5% of their average net profit. Under the new scheme: unlimited.

### The Supreme Court's Verdict — "Unconstitutional"

On 15 February 2024, a five-judge bench of the Supreme Court of India, headed by Chief Justice DY Chandrachud, unanimously struck down the Electoral Bond Scheme as unconstitutional. They found it "violative of the Right to Information" and of voters' right to information about political funding under Article 19(1)(a) of the Constitution.

The court said the system is "unconstitutional" and that the close nexus between money and politics meant financial contributions "would lead to quid pro quo arrangements."

The Supreme Court did not mince words. In its ruling, the Supreme Court observed, "This scenario fosters crony capitalism, a threat to India's democracy."

India's highest court — not an opposition party, not a foreign government — called the BJP's flagship political funding scheme a threat to Indian democracy.

### Where the Money Went

When the data was finally published, the numbers were staggering.

The initial issuance of electoral bonds saw the BJP acquire approximately 95 percent of the total bonds, as per information obtained through Right to Information requests and BJP submissions.

While the BJP raked in about $788 million from electoral bonds, the most prominent opposition party, the Indian National Congress, managed to secure only $134 million.

The game was rigged by design. The BJP, as the ruling party, had access to information about who was buying the bonds — giving it enormous leverage over potential donors. Critics posit that the ruling party's exclusive access to donor information dissuades potential contributors from channelling their funds to opposition parties out of fear or intimidation.

### The Quid Pro Quo Pattern

When the bond data was finally released and cross-referenced, investigative reporters found a pattern that investigators say appears to be direct corruption.

Rajya Sabha Member Kapil Sibal highlighted apparent instances of quid pro quo, suggesting that individuals or entities under investigation by agencies like the Enforcement Directorate or CBI made donations through the Electoral Bonds scheme, after which no further action was taken against them. The data revealed that over 15 of the top 30 companies that donated to political parties through the scheme were subjects of investigation by these agencies, with investigations ranging in intensity from case filings to premises raids.

The pattern: company gets raided by ED or CBI. Company buys electoral bonds and donates to BJP. Investigation quietly stops.

The biggest single donor was Future Gaming and Hotels Pvt Ltd, run by Santiago Martin. This lottery company purchased bonds worth ₹1,300 crore — and of these, bonds worth ₹100 crore were purchased seven days after a raid by India's Enforcement Directorate over charges of money laundering.

A company raids. Then buys bonds. Raids stop. This is the documented pattern.

The SBI itself tried to delay releasing the data — the SBI failed to submit the details by the court-ordered deadline and approached the Supreme Court asking for more time. The court turned down this request.

India's government-owned bank, protecting political funding data, defying the Supreme Court's own deadline — until the court forced it to comply.

## Part 3: PM CARES Fund — ₹10,000 Crore and Zero Accountability

### The Fund That Cannot Be Questioned

On March 28, 2020 — three days before the end of the financial year, at the peak of COVID-19 panic — the Modi government created a new fund: PM CARES (Prime Minister's Citizen Assistance and Relief in Emergency Situations).

The justification: a dedicated fund to fight COVID-19.

The problem: India already had a dedicated fund for exactly this purpose. The Prime Minister's National Relief Fund (PMNRF) had existed since 1948, specifically for emergencies. It was subject to CAG audit and parliamentary scrutiny.

There is no clarity on why it was created when the statutorily created Prime Minister's Relief Fund existed for that purpose.

Within days, the government:
- Directed PSUs (public sector companies), banks, and universities to donate to PM CARES
- Declared it a valid Corporate Social Responsibility destination
- Deducted one day's salary from all government employees and deposited it into PM CARES
- Exempted it from the Foreign Contribution Regulation Act — allowing foreign money to pour in without any documentation

Thousands of crores flooded in. At least ₹9,677.9 crore was collected, based on analysis of government press releases and media reports. At least ₹4,308.3 crore came from government agencies and staff, and at least ₹438.8 crore was deducted from the one-day salaries of government employees.

### The Fraud at the Heart of PM CARES

Here is the central contradiction — documented in court filings:

When it came to collecting money, PM CARES was touted as a fund set up by the central government. But when people started asking for information under the RTI Act, the government said it was not set up by the central government and would not be brought under RTI as a public authority.

Government when collecting your money: *"This is a national government fund. Please donate."*

Government when you ask where the money went: *"This is a private trust. RTI does not apply."*

The Government of India has asserted that it is a public body, controlled by the Government of India, but is still not subject to transparency laws like the RTI Act.

The Prime Minister has been the Chairperson of the Fund and senior Cabinet ministers its trustees. It is run out of the PMO, uses government infrastructure, and sports the national emblem — yet the government has refused to subject it to RTI queries or to a CAG audit.

The fund uses the national emblem of India. It is chaired by the Prime Minister in his official capacity. It collected money from government employees' salaries and government-owned companies. And yet it cannot be audited by India's Comptroller and Auditor General, and no RTI question about it will be answered.

### Parliament Cannot Even Ask

The government did not stop at blocking RTI. A PMO communication to the Lok Sabha Secretariat has reportedly made questions on PM CARES not admissible under House rules.

India's elected Members of Parliament — whose job is to hold the executive accountable — have been told they are not allowed to ask questions about PM CARES in Parliament.

### The Audit Blackhole

Since 2022, there are no audit reports of the PM CARES Fund in the public domain. Either audits are not happening, or audits are happening but not being published. Where are the audit reports? Is it above the law?

Transparency activist Anjali Bhardwaj, who has filed multiple RTI applications on PM CARES, put it plainly: *"This is nothing but a fraud that is being played on the people of this country. There is a clear attempt to hide what is happening in the PM CARES Fund."*

The government's answer to every question: silence.

## The Pattern — This Is Not Coincidence

Look at these three structures together:

**Adani:** An opaque corporate empire built through offshore structures so complex that India's own regulators "hit a wall." American prosecutors indicted the chairman for allegedly bribing Indian officials. India's response: nothing.

**Electoral Bonds:** A political funding system where companies under investigation by government agencies donated to the ruling party, after which investigations stopped. The Supreme Court called it unconstitutional and a tool for crony capitalism.

**PM CARES:** ₹10,000 crore collected in the government's name from government employees and government companies, placed in a fund that calls itself private when transparency is demanded, with no CAG audit, no RTI, and no Parliament allowed to ask questions.

These are not separate scandals. They are three pillars of the same architecture:

**Collect money in the dark. Spend it without accountability. Punish anyone who asks.**

This is not normal governance. This is not the way the Constitution of India imagined its democracy working.

Ambedkar wrote the Constitution so that power would be accountable to the people. The Supreme Court struck down Electoral Bonds to protect that principle. American prosecutors filed charges to expose corporate fraud. International journalists documented the offshore structures that Indian regulators could not penetrate.

The accountability came from outside. From courts, from foreign prosecutors, from investigative journalists. Not from the government. Not from the party. Never voluntarily.

**In a democracy, the people own the government. In Modi's India, the question of who owns what has become very difficult to answer — and asking it is becoming harder every year.**
