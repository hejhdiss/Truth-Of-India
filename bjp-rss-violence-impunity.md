# The State of Impunity: How BJP and RSS Enable Violence Against Women and Minorities

*A Critical Investigative Report | Sources: Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), Reuters, The Wire, Al Jazeera, BBC, Scroll.in, Article 14, People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL), Frontline, The Caravan, UN Special Rapporteurs*

---

## Introduction

In a functioning democracy, the state is the last line of defence for the most vulnerable — the woman who has been assaulted, the minority family whose home has been torched, the Dalit child whose humanity is denied. In India under BJP and RSS rule, the documented evidence shows that the state has repeatedly become something else entirely: a shield for perpetrators, a weapon against victims, and an architecture of silence around crimes that embarrass those in power.

This is not an allegation. It is a pattern — confirmed by India's own courts, by the National Crime Records Bureau, by UN Special Rapporteurs, and by the testimonies of survivors whose voices the government has worked systematically to suppress.

This article documents three interlocking crises: the epidemic of rape and sexual violence and the BJP government's record of protecting accused perpetrators; the documented pattern of organised communal violence against minorities under BJP-ruled states; and the institutionalised impunity that has made the police, courts, and central enforcement agencies instruments of the ruling party rather than servants of justice.

---

## Part 1: Rape, Sexual Violence, and the Culture of Impunity

### The Scale of the Crisis

India has one of the highest rates of recorded sexual violence among major democracies. According to the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), a rape is reported in India **every 16 minutes**. In 2022 alone, 31,516 rape cases were registered — and criminologists consistently note that the actual number is many times higher, as most victims never report, knowing the system will fail them.

The NCRB data reveals another devastating truth: **in over 95% of reported rape cases, the accused was known to the victim** — a family member, neighbour, or authority figure. This is not random street crime. It is structural violence enabled by social hierarchies that the RSS's Hindutva ideology has historically reinforced — the subordination of women to male authority, the caste hierarchy that treats Dalit women as available for upper-caste violation, the communal targeting of minority women in times of conflict.

### The Hathras Case: State as Perpetrator

On September 14, 2020, a 19-year-old Dalit woman in Hathras, Uttar Pradesh — governed by BJP's Yogi Adityanath — was gang-raped, tortured, and left for dead by four upper-caste men in a field. She survived long enough to name her attackers. She died from her injuries on September 29, 2020.

What happened next was not a failure of justice. It was an active assault on justice by the state itself.

In the early hours of September 30, while her family slept inside their home under police guard, local officials cremated her body **without her family's consent**, in darkness, at 2 AM. Her family was not permitted to hold her, bathe her, or perform last rites. The cremation destroyed critical forensic evidence. UP Police subsequently issued a statement — widely condemned by medical experts and the victim's family — claiming her medical examination showed "no rape."

Uttar Pradesh authorities then filed sedition charges against journalists who reported the story. A Kerala journalist, Siddique Kappan, travelling to Hathras to cover the case, was arrested under the draconian Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) and held for over two years without trial. Chief Minister Yogi Adityanath did not visit the family. He did not apologise. His administration's response was to manage optics and criminalise coverage.

The Allahabad High Court later took suo motu cognisance of the case and described the UP administration's conduct as deeply troubling. In 2023, a sessions court **acquitted all four accused** — a verdict that the victim's family and rights groups described as a catastrophic failure of the criminal justice system.

### The Manipur Sexual Violence: Filmed, Reported, Ignored

On May 4, 2023, ethnic violence erupted in Manipur — a BJP-governed state. In the chaos, a video emerged showing a group of men parading two Kuki-Zo women naked through a field, groping them, while a crowd watched. The video was filmed by a participant. It was one of multiple incidents of sexual violence against Kuki women documented during the violence.

The video existed for **77 days** before Prime Minister Modi said a single word about it publicly — and only then after it went viral internationally and Parliament erupted in outrage.

Human Rights Watch documented multiple incidents of sexual violence against women from the Kuki-Zo community during the Manipur conflict, noting that "survivors have faced intimidation and lack of access to justice." Amnesty International called for independent investigations. The UN Special Rapporteur on Violence Against Women wrote to the Indian government expressing grave concern.

As of 2024, the ethnic violence in Manipur had displaced over **60,000 people** and killed more than 200. The internet shutdown imposed by the BJP state government — one of India's longest — ensured that for months, the world could not see what was happening. When the video finally broke through, the government's response was to question its circulation, not to address the violence it documented.

### The Wrestlers' Protest: When Victims Are the Criminals

In April 2023, India's most celebrated women wrestlers — Olympic and Commonwealth Games medallists Vinesh Phogat, Bajrang Punia, and Sakshi Malik — sat in protest at Jantar Mantar in New Delhi. They accused Brij Bhushan Sharan Singh, the BJP Member of Parliament and Wrestling Federation of India chief, of serial sexual harassment of female athletes, including a minor.

The Delhi Police — which reports directly to the BJP-led central government, not to Delhi's elected state government — took months to act. Singh was not arrested. He continued to attend BJP events and was publicly defended by party members. When the wrestlers attempted to march to the new Parliament building to deposit a complaint, **they were detained by Delhi Police** — India's Olympic champions, in their own country's capital, arrested for asking that a rape accusation be investigated.

An FIR was eventually filed. Singh was given anticipatory bail. He was subsequently fielded by the BJP as a candidate in the 2024 general elections. He won.

Vinesh Phogat, one of the primary complainants, later joined the Congress party. The BJP's response was to suggest her complaint had been politically motivated all along — a complete inversion: the accused runs for Parliament, the accuser is called a political agent.

## Part 2: Communal Violence and the Majoritarian State

### The Architecture of Organised Violence

The violence that periodically convulses BJP-ruled states is not spontaneous. Human Rights Watch, People's Union for Civil Liberties, and multiple academic researchers have documented a consistent pattern: communal riots in India follow a recognisable template — mobilisation through inflammatory speeches by Hindutva leaders, attacks on minority neighbourhoods, targeting of mosques and churches, and a police force that either stands aside, arrives late, or in documented cases, participates.

This pattern was visible in **Delhi, February 2020**, when anti-Muslim violence killed at least 53 people, destroyed mosques, and displaced hundreds of families from the Northeast Delhi neighbourhood. The violence followed months of BJP leaders making inflammatory speeches about anti-CAA protesters — Home Minister Amit Shah asking crowds to "press the button [vote] with such anger that the current felt here is felt in Shaheen Bagh." Video footage showed police personnel standing alongside mobs in some locations. Arrests were made almost exclusively of Muslim victims and protesters, not attackers.

The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights, Michelle Bachelet, called the Delhi violence "deeply concerning." Human Rights Watch documented how the Delhi Police — controlled by the Union Home Ministry under Amit Shah — failed to respond adequately and in some cases facilitated violence. An Indian court later acquitted most of the accused attackers.

### Bulldozer Justice: The Demolition State

Under BJP rule, a new form of collective punishment has emerged that has been condemned by India's own Supreme Court: the use of state machinery to demolish the homes and properties of Muslims accused — not convicted — of crimes or protest activity.

The practice became so common under UP Chief Minister Yogi Adityanath that his government began publicly celebrating it. The bulldozer became a BJP campaign symbol. "Bulldozer Baba" became Adityanath's unofficial title, worn with pride.

In June 2022, following communal tensions in Prayagraj, UP authorities demolished the home of Javed Mohammad — whose daughter Afreen Fatima had been a prominent anti-government protester. No court order authorised the demolition. The family had not been convicted of anything. The demolition happened within 24 hours of the protests, before any legal process could occur. This is the definition of extrajudicial collective punishment.

In October 2024, a Constitution Bench of India's Supreme Court ruled that demolitions carried out without due process were **unconstitutional**, and issued guidelines requiring notice before any demolition. The Court's ruling was an implicit indictment of years of systematic abuse — but by that point, hundreds of homes, mostly belonging to Muslims, had already been erased.

### Anti-Conversion Laws and the Criminalisation of Interfaith Love

Across BJP-ruled states — Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand — "anti-conversion" laws have been passed that Human Rights Watch describes as targeting interfaith couples, predominantly Hindu women with Muslim partners, under the pretext of combating "Love Jihad" — a conspiracy theory with no basis in any official data.

Human Rights Watch's 2023 report documented how these laws are used specifically to intimidate and break up interfaith relationships, with police arresting Muslim men — sometimes on the complaint of the woman's family — even when the woman herself states no pressure was applied. Women who are adults and in consensual relationships are treated as incapable of making their own choices, their bodies and choices becoming sites of state control.

The Allahabad High Court in 2020 made a landmark ruling that a person's right to choose a partner cannot be subjected to scrutiny by the state or family. The UP government responded by immediately changing the law to expand its reach.

## Part 3: The Institutional Collapse — When the Police Serve the Party

### The Selective Enforcement Machine

The evidence for institutional collapse is not anecdotal. It is statistical and systematic.

A Scroll investigation found that **95% of Enforcement Directorate cases since 2014 were filed against Opposition leaders**. The same pattern applies to crimes of communal violence: arrests are made overwhelmingly against victims of violence who dared to protest, not against the mobs that perpetrated it.

In case after case, the police response to anti-Muslim violence follows a documented pattern:
- **FIRs are filed against Muslim victims** who attempted to defend themselves or document attacks
- **Perpetrators from majority communities** receive bail quickly or are not arrested at all
- **Witnesses are intimidated** and in multiple documented cases, pressured to retract statements
- **Forensic evidence is mishandled** or, as in Hathras, actively destroyed

This is not the natural inefficiency of an overburdened system. It is the systematic application of police power in one direction.

### UAPA: The Lock with No Key

The Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act has become, under BJP governance, a tool to silence those who document or protest against state violence. It allows detention without bail for years before trial, and bail is nearly impossible to obtain once the charge is applied.

The Wire, Article 14, and Amnesty International have documented a consistent pattern: **UAPA charges are disproportionately applied to Muslim activists, Dalit rights organisers, journalists covering communal violence, and academics who criticise BJP-RSS ideology.** Stan Swamy, an 83-year-old Jesuit priest and Adivasi rights activist, was arrested under UAPA in 2020 and died in judicial custody in 2021 while his bail application was still pending — he had Parkinson's disease and was denied even a sipper cup to drink water for weeks.

Father Stan Swamy was never convicted. He was never tried. He died in jail.

### The Courts: Justice Delayed, Justice Denied

India's judiciary retains significant independence and has delivered landmark rulings — the Supreme Court's striking down of Electoral Bonds, its ruling against demolitions without due process. But the lower courts and the trial process itself have become instruments of delay and attrition for cases involving powerful accused.

In the Bilkis Bano case — one of the most horrific gang rapes of the 2002 Gujarat riots, in which 14 people including Bilkis's three-year-old daughter were murdered — the eleven men convicted of gang rape and murder served only 15 years before the BJP-led Gujarat government granted them early **remission** of sentence in August 2022. They were released to garland-wearing celebrations by VHP members. Senior BJP politicians praised the decision.

India's Supreme Court later quashed this remission, calling the Gujarat government's action unconstitutional and ordering the convicts back to jail. But the sequence of events tells the entire story: a BJP state government freed convicted gang rapists, held victory celebrations, and was only reversed by the Supreme Court more than a year later, after national and international outrage.

## Part 4: The RSS Ideological Foundation

### What Golwalkar Actually Said

This pattern of violence and impunity does not emerge from a vacuum. The RSS's foundational text — *We or Our Nationhood Defined* by M.S. Golwalkar, written in 1939 — contains passages that explicitly admire Nazi Germany's racial laws as a model for India's "national regeneration." Golwalkar wrote approvingly of how Germany showed "how well-nigh impossible it is for races and cultures, having differences going to the root, to be assimilated into one united whole."

More directly relevant to sexual violence: RSS ideology has historically framed women as vessels of the nation's honour — to be protected by Hindu men, but only within the parameters of caste and community. This framing — women as community property rather than autonomous individuals — is the ideological soil in which "Love Jihad" conspiracy theories, anti-conversion laws targeting interfaith women, and the violent policing of interfaith couples grow.

It is also the soil in which the repeated defence of upper-caste perpetrators against Dalit victims grows. When upper-caste men rape Dalit women, the RSS social hierarchy places the perpetrator above the victim — not legally, but culturally. The documented behaviour of BJP-governed state police in cases like Hathras and Unnao reflects this hierarchy in action.

### The BJP's Record with Its Own Accused

The clearest test of a party's genuine commitment to women's safety is how it treats accused individuals within its own ranks. The BJP's record is unambiguous:

- **Brij Bhushan Sharan Singh** — accused of serial sexual harassment including of a minor by India's Olympic champions. Not arrested. Fielded as BJP candidate. Elected.
- **Kuldeep Sengar** — BJP MLA from Unnao, convicted of raping a minor in 2019. He was fielded in elections even after arrest, expelled from party only after Supreme Court intervention, convicted by a fast-track court only after the victim's father died in suspicious custody and the victim herself survived a suspicious road accident.
- **Swami Chinmayanand** — BJP-aligned leader accused of raping an LLM student. The student who filed the complaint was arrested first, on extortion charges, before any action was taken against him.

The pattern is consistent: accusation leads to protection, victim-blaming, and in multiple cases, the criminalisation of the complainant.

## Conclusion: What Impunity Costs

India cannot build a just society on a foundation of protected violence. Every rape that is covered up, every demolished home, every journalist arrested for covering a crime, every wrestler detained for demanding an investigation — these are not isolated incidents. They are the operating system of a government that has decided certain people's safety does not matter.

The cost is borne most heavily by the most vulnerable: Dalit women, Muslim families, tribal communities, Adivasi rights workers, and the millions of ordinary Indians — Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Christian, women of every community — who have simply learned not to call the police, because they know who the police work for.

The documented record presented here is not opposition politics. It comes from India's own Supreme Court, from NCRB data, from convictions in Indian courts, from UN Special Rapporteur letters, from peer-reviewed research, and from the testimonies of survivors. Every claim can be verified. Every source is listed.

The question for every Indian is simple: **Is this the country you were told you lived in?**
